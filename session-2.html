Treaties & Broken Promises

How sacred agreements between nations became instruments of dispossession — the story of treaty-making, systemic betrayal, and the Indian Act’s enduring shadow over Indigenous life in Canada.

Why Treaties Felt “Good” at First

Indigenous leaders sought a survivable future in a permanently changed world

One of the most persistent myths in Canadian history is that Indigenous leaders who signed treaties were somehow naive or duped into surrendering their land. The reality is far more complex and far more respectful of Indigenous intelligence. By the time the Numbered Treaties were being negotiated in the 1870s, Indigenous nations had been engaging in diplomacy with European powers for centuries. They were deeply experienced negotiators who understood the shifting balance of power on the continent.

What had changed was the world itself. The great buffalo herds that had sustained Plains nations for millennia were in catastrophic decline, driven to near-extinction by commercial hunting and settler expansion. The fur trade that had structured economic relationships for two centuries was collapsing. Disease had swept through communities repeatedly, devastating populations that had no immunity to European illnesses. Indigenous leaders looked at this landscape and made pragmatic calculations about survival.

What they wanted from treaties was concrete and reasonable: farming tools and livestock to transition to agriculture, education that would give their children access to what some leaders called “the cunning of the white man,” healthcare to combat the diseases ravaging their people, and legal protections for their children and future generations. These were not the demands of a defeated people surrendering unconditionally. These were the terms of nations seeking partnership.

Crucially, Indigenous nations understood treaty-making as a sacred act. The process involved ceremony, the sharing of gifts, the smoking of the pipe, the exchange of wampum, and extensive verbal commitments. From the Indigenous perspective, these ceremonies were not decorative additions to a legal proceeding — they were the ratification itself. The words spoken in ceremony, the commitments made before the Creator, were the most binding form of law that existed. The “spirit and intent” of what was promised mattered more than any marks on paper.

Key Concept

Spirit and Intent of Treaty

In Indigenous legal traditions, the “spirit and intent” of an agreement refers to the full meaning of what was discussed, promised, and ceremonially affirmed — not merely what was written in English on paper. This concept remains central to modern treaty interpretation and has been increasingly recognized by Canadian courts as essential to understanding what was actually agreed to.

02

Two Ships Passing in the Night

The fundamental clash of legal and moral frameworks

At the heart of every treaty negotiation lay a profound and irreconcilable clash of worldviews. Indigenous nations and the British Crown were using the same word — “treaty” — to describe fundamentally different things. Understanding this mismatch is essential to understanding everything that followed, because it was not an accident. The ambiguity served the Crown’s purposes perfectly.

For Indigenous nations, a treaty was a relationship. It established a bond between peoples, a commitment to share the land and its resources in ways that would sustain everyone. It was a moral and spiritual obligation, not merely a legal contract. Verbal commitments were binding — more so than written text, because words spoken before witnesses and before the Creator carried the weight of sacred law. The ceremony surrounding treaty negotiations — pipe ceremonies, gift exchanges, feasting — was the ratification process. Indigenous law recognized these commitments as the highest form of obligation.

For the Crown, a treaty was a land transaction. It was a mechanism for acquiring territory, creating legal certainty for settlers and investors, and establishing jurisdictional control. The only “real” contract was the written document, drafted in English (and sometimes French), using legal terminology that treaty commissioners controlled. Enforcement would happen through colonial courts, under colonial law, interpreted by colonial judges. The goal was not relationship but control — clearing the legal path for settlement, resource extraction, and the expansion of the Dominion.

The Crown understood treaties as land acquisition. Indigenous nations understood them as sacred relationships. That built-in mismatch was not a failure of communication — it was a feature the Crown benefited from.

This framework clash meant that Indigenous leaders left treaty negotiations believing they had entered into a sacred, ongoing relationship of sharing, mutual respect, and mutual obligation. Crown negotiators left believing they had acquired the land outright, with Indigenous peoples consenting to move onto small reserves in exchange for modest annual payments. Both parties could point to the treaty process and say agreement had been reached — but they had agreed to entirely different things.

Legal Precedent

Honour of the Crown Doctrine

Haida Nation v. British Columbia, 2004 SCC 73

The Supreme Court of Canada has recognized that treaties must be interpreted in light of the “honour of the Crown,” meaning the government has an obligation to act honourably in all dealings with Indigenous peoples. Ambiguities in treaties must be resolved in favour of Indigenous peoples, and the Crown cannot hide behind the narrow wording of documents its own agents drafted. This principle acknowledges what Indigenous nations have always known: the spirit and intent of the treaty is what matters.

03

What People Actually Got

Delivery never matched the spirit and intent of what was promised

The gap between what was promised and what was delivered became apparent almost immediately. Complaints about treaty implementation began, in many cases, “the day after” the signing. Reserves were smaller than what had been discussed in oral negotiations. Farm implements that were supposed to help communities transition to agriculture were withheld, delayed, or arrived in unusable condition. The schools that were promised — meant to give children skills for the new economy — would eventually become the residential school system, one of the greatest catastrophes in Canadian history.

Cash annuities became one of the most visible symbols of the treaty relationship’s failure. Treaty 1, signed on August 3, 1871, at Lower Fort Garry, established an annuity of five dollars per person per year. That amount has never been adjusted. To this day, Treaty 1 beneficiaries are entitled to receive five dollars annually — an amount that was already modest in 1871 and is now so small as to function as an insult rather than a benefit. For many Indigenous people, the annual treaty payment ceremony is a painful reminder of broken faith, even as it also serves as an assertion of the continuing treaty relationship.

Primary Source

Treaty 1 (Stone Fort Treaty) — Annuity Provision

Signed August 3, 1871, Lower Fort Garry, Manitoba

“Her Majesty agrees and undertakes to pay to each Indian person the sum of five dollars per head yearly… and further agrees to maintain a school on each reserve hereby made whenever the Indians of the reserve shall desire it.”

The issue of “outside promises” has haunted treaty interpretation ever since. These were commitments made orally during negotiations that did not appear in the final written text. In many cases, treaty commissioners made specific promises about hunting rights, the size of reserves, the quality and quantity of tools and animals, and the nature of education that would be provided. These promises were understood by Indigenous leaders as part of the treaty itself. When the written documents did not include them, the Crown claimed they were not binding.

Modern treaty disputes often hinge on the challenge of translating the language and commitments of 1871 into contemporary legal frameworks. What did “as long as the sun shines and the rivers flow” mean in legal terms? What was the precise nature of the “sharing” that was discussed? Indigenous oral histories preserve detailed accounts of what was promised, and Canadian courts have increasingly recognized these accounts as legitimate evidence — but the process of reconciling two fundamentally different record-keeping systems remains fraught with difficulty.

Key Concept

Outside Promises

“Outside promises” refers to commitments made orally by Crown treaty commissioners during negotiations that were not included in the final written treaty text. These verbal commitments were considered binding by Indigenous signatories, who understood the spoken word — especially words spoken in ceremony — as carrying the highest legal authority. The exclusion of these promises from written documents has been a central source of dispute in treaty interpretation for over 150 years.

04

The Crown’s Leverage Was Life-or-Death

Coercion, starvation, and disease as instruments of policy

Understanding why treaties did not produce the outcomes Indigenous leaders negotiated for requires understanding the tools of coercion the Crown deployed after agreements were signed. The federal government did not simply fail to honour its commitments through negligence or lack of resources. It deliberately used the withholding of promised provisions as a weapon of control, turning dependence into a mechanism of power.

The tools were blunt and effective. Food rations that were supposed to sustain communities through the transition from hunting to farming were withheld from bands that resisted government directives. Agricultural tools were denied or delayed. Weapons were seized, not merely as a security measure, but to eliminate the ability of communities to feed themselves through hunting. Permit systems were introduced requiring Indigenous people to obtain written permission from an Indian Agent to leave their reserve, sell their crops, or conduct basic economic activity. The government created dependency by destroying existing means of self-sufficiency, and then policed that dependency through bureaucratic control.

When people are starving and sick, “consent” becomes coerced. The Crown did not simply break its promises — it weaponized the breaking.

— Indigenous History: A Path Forward, Episode 2

Edgar Dewdney, who served as Indian Commissioner and later Lieutenant Governor of the North-West Territories in the 1880s, implemented a formal system of reward and punishment. Bands that cooperated with government directives — sending children to school, accepting reserve boundaries, ceasing to travel or gather in large groups — received their rations and supplies. Bands that resisted or complained had provisions cut. This was not a side effect of bureaucratic incompetence; it was deliberate policy, documented in government correspondence and reports.

Disease compounded coercion into catastrophe. Smallpox epidemics had already devastated many Indigenous communities before treaties were signed. After treaties, tuberculosis, influenza, and other infectious diseases continued to sweep through crowded, under-resourced reserves. The government was aware of the health crisis and understood that its own policies — confining people to small reserves with inadequate food, shelter, and medical care — were contributing to mass death. The weakened state of communities made further resistance nearly impossible, and the Crown’s leverage grew with every epidemic, every famine, every season of deprivation.

Historical Record

The Dewdney System of Control

Edgar Dewdney, Indian Commissioner, North-West Territories, 1879–1888

Government records from the 1880s document a formal policy of using food rations as leverage over treaty nations on the prairies. Indian Commissioner Edgar Dewdney explicitly linked the distribution of government-promised rations to compliance with government directives. Bands that resisted the pass system, refused to send children to industrial schools, or attempted to organize politically found their supplies cut — a practice that amounted to using starvation as a tool of colonial governance.

05

Indian Lands Were Open for Business

Resource extraction then and now — the constant through Canadian history

If the treaty process had a single underlying economic logic, it was this: Indigenous lands contained resources that settlers and their governments wanted access to. Treaties were the mechanism for clearing the legal title — or at least creating the appearance of legal title — so that mining, logging, hydroelectric development, and farming could proceed without the inconvenience of Indigenous ownership claims. The language of treaties spoke of sharing and mutual benefit, but the practice was extraction.

In the decades following the Numbered Treaties, resource industries moved onto Indigenous territories with little or no consultation, compensation, or concern for the communities affected. Mining operations contaminated waterways. Logging stripped forests that had sustained Indigenous economies for millennia. Hydroelectric dams flooded traditional territories, displacing communities and destroying fisheries. Agricultural settlement carved up hunting grounds into private property. In every case, the wealth generated flowed to non-Indigenous individuals, corporations, and governments, while Indigenous communities bore the environmental and social costs.

What makes this history particularly important is how directly it connects to the present. The fundamental conflict has not changed. Modern resource disputes — pipelines, mining, forestry, hydroelectric development — still centre on the same questions: Who controls access to resources on Indigenous territories? Who benefits from that extraction? And whose consent is required before development proceeds? The language has shifted from “clearing title for settlement” to “certainty for third-party investors,” but the underlying logic remains remarkably consistent.

Key Concept

Certainty for Third-Party Investors

In contemporary resource development, governments and industry frequently argue that clear, settled land rights are necessary to provide “certainty for third-party investors.” Critics note that this language is functionally identical to the original treaty rationale: clearing Indigenous title so that non-Indigenous interests can access resources with legal confidence. The question of whose certainty matters — and at whose expense — remains at the centre of Indigenous-Crown relations in Canada.

The economic relationship established in the treaty era set a pattern that persists: Indigenous peoples live on some of the most resource-rich land in Canada while experiencing some of the highest poverty rates. The extraction of wealth from Indigenous territories has been one of the foundations of Canadian prosperity. Understanding this is not about assigning historical guilt — it is about recognizing that the economic structure of contemporary Canada was built, in significant part, on the systematic dispossession of Indigenous nations from their resource base.

06

The Indian Act (1876) — The Enforcement Machine

Canada’s apartheid document and the architecture of control

If treaties were the framework through which the Crown acquired legal claim to Indigenous territories, the Indian Act was the mechanism through which it controlled the people who lived on them. Passed in 1876 — just five years after Treaty 1 — the Indian Act consolidated earlier colonial legislation into a single, comprehensive statute that governed virtually every aspect of Indigenous life. It has been called many things: a “racist document,” “our apartheid document,” and a global outlier as a piece of legislation that explicitly targets a specific racial group for differential treatment under the law.

The Act’s reach was extraordinary. It installed Indian Agents on reserves as the government’s direct representatives, wielding near-absolute authority over the daily lives of Indigenous people. It replaced Indigenous governance systems — many of which had operated for centuries with sophisticated structures of consensus, clan governance, and hereditary leadership — with elected band councils modelled on colonial municipal governments. It stripped Indigenous women of their status if they married non-Indigenous men, severing their legal connection to their communities and their rights under treaty. It created the reserve system and dictated how reserve land could be used, transferred, or surrendered.

Primary Source

Indian Act, 1876 — Section on Enfranchisement

An Act to amend and consolidate the laws respecting Indians, S.C. 1876, c. 18

“The term ‘person’ means an individual other than an Indian…” — This definition, embedded in early Canadian law, legally excluded Indigenous people from the category of “persons,” denying them the rights, protections, and legal standing available to all other inhabitants of the country.

Among the Act’s most destructive provisions were the cultural bans. Beginning in 1884, the potlatch — the cornerstone of West Coast Indigenous governance, economy, and spiritual life — was made a criminal offence. The sun dance and thirst dance, sacred ceremonies central to Plains nations, were similarly banned. These were not minor cultural practices being restricted; they were the institutional foundations of Indigenous societies. Banning them was the legal equivalent of outlawing Parliament, the stock exchange, and church services simultaneously. The intent was to destroy the social structures that held Indigenous communities together.

The Act also mandated residential schooling, giving the government authority to remove children from their families and communities and place them in church-run institutions far from home. It banned Indigenous people from raising money to pursue land claims, and in 1927 it went further, making it illegal for Indigenous people to hire lawyers to press their grievances. This combination — stripping people of their governance, their ceremonies, their children, and their legal recourse — was not a collection of unrelated policies. It was a system, deliberately designed to achieve a stated goal.

Primary Source

Duncan Campbell Scott’s Statement to Parliament

Deputy Superintendent General of Indian Affairs, 1920

“I want to get rid of the Indian problem. I do not think as a matter of fact, that the country ought to continuously protect a class of people who are able to stand alone… Our objective is to continue until there is not a single Indian in Canada that has not been absorbed into the body politic and there is no Indian question, and no Indian Department.”

Duncan Campbell Scott’s 1920 statement to Parliament laid bare the logic that animated the Indian Act from its inception. The “Indian problem” he sought to eliminate was not poverty or marginalization — it was the existence of Indigenous identity itself. Every provision of the Act, from the cultural bans to the residential schools to the prohibition on legal advocacy, was oriented toward a single objective: the elimination of Indigenous peoples as distinct nations, cultures, and legal entities within Canada.

Legal Framework

Key Indian Act Prohibitions

Selected provisions, Indian Act amendments 1876–1951

1884: Potlatch and West Coast ceremonial practices banned (punishable by imprisonment). 1895: Sun Dance and Thirst Dance banned on the Plains. 1920: Compulsory school attendance mandated, empowering the government to forcibly remove children. 1927: Raising funds or retaining lawyers for the purpose of pursuing land claims made illegal. These prohibitions, stacked together over decades, constituted a systematic dismantling of Indigenous self-governance, cultural practice, and legal capacity.

07

Why the Indian Act Still Exists

Dependency, fear, and the absence of alternatives

If the Indian Act is as harmful as described — and virtually every Indigenous leader, legal scholar, and human rights body that has examined it agrees that it is — the obvious question is: why hasn’t it been repealed? The answer reveals one of the most insidious aspects of the system it created. The Act engineered dependency so effectively that removing it, without building something better to replace it, could cause more harm than leaving it in place.

Generations of economic marginalization, the destruction of traditional governance structures, and the systematic dismantling of self-sufficiency have left many communities in a position where the Indian Act’s provisions, however degrading their origin, represent the only existing framework for essential services. Tax exemptions for on-reserve income, however limited in practice, are seen as one of the few remaining protections against further economic erosion. Reserve land, held in trust by the Crown under the Act, is protected from seizure and sale in ways that other land is not — and Indigenous communities are acutely aware that in the absence of such protection, their remaining land base would be vulnerable to market pressures.

Poverty and dependency produce fear: “Who is going to look after us?” The system created the dependency, and then pointed to it as proof that the system was needed.

— Indigenous History: A Path Forward, Episode 2

The structural familiarity of the Indian Act also plays a role. Band council elections, the administration of reserves, the delivery of federal services — all of these are organized around the Act’s framework. Replacing it requires not just political will but the design and implementation of entirely new governance structures, negotiated community by community, nation by nation. It requires substantial investment in capacity-building, the resolution of outstanding land claims, and the establishment of self-governance frameworks that reflect the diversity of Indigenous nations across Canada.

This is why Indigenous leaders generally do not call for simple repeal. What they call for is replacement — a process of building new legal and governance frameworks that restore self-determination while protecting existing rights. Repeal without replacement would create a legal vacuum, stripping away even the minimal protections the Act provides while offering nothing in return. The path forward is not the elimination of the Indian Act in a single legislative act, but its gradual displacement by self-government agreements, modern treaties, and nation-to-nation frameworks that make its provisions unnecessary.

Key Concept

Repeal vs. Replacement

The critical distinction in Indian Act reform is between repeal (simply removing the legislation) and replacement (building alternative legal frameworks before the Act is removed). Simple repeal without replacement would create a legal vacuum that could strip Indigenous communities of land protections, tax provisions, and service delivery mechanisms without providing alternatives. Meaningful reform requires the construction of self-government agreements, modern treaties, and nation-to-nation frameworks that progressively make the Act’s provisions unnecessary.

08

Change Through Modern Treaties & Self-Government

From engineered dependence to reclaimed autonomy

Despite the weight of history, the story of treaties in Canada is not only one of betrayal. It is also a story of resilience and ongoing transformation. Modern treaties and self-government agreements represent a fundamentally different approach to the Indigenous-Crown relationship — one that, at its best, reflects the original spirit of treaty-making as a relationship between sovereign peoples.

The James Bay and Northern Quebec Agreement of 1975 marked a turning point as the first major modern land claim agreement in Canada. It was followed by a series of comprehensive agreements that have reshaped governance across the country. The creation of Nunavut in 1999 — a new territory with an Inuit-majority population and government designed to reflect Inuit values and priorities — demonstrated that fundamentally different governance structures were possible within Confederation. The Nisga’a Final Agreement of 2000, the first modern treaty in British Columbia, established self-government powers that include control over citizenship, culture, language, land, and resources.

At the community level, custom-of-band elections have allowed many First Nations to move away from the Indian Act’s imposed electoral system and toward governance models rooted in their own traditions. Cultural bans that once criminalized Indigenous spiritual practice were lifted over the course of the twentieth century — the potlatch ban ended in 1951, though its effects persisted for generations. Ceremonies that were driven underground have been revived, and the cultural knowledge that survived decades of suppression is being actively transmitted to new generations.

Yet the personal cost of the system’s legacy remains raw. Phil Fontaine, among others, has spoken about the humiliation of welfare gatekeeping on reserves — the experience of being forced to justify every purchase, explain every expense, and submit to the judgment of non-Indigenous administrators in order to access basic necessities. The system was engineered to produce dependence, and then it used that dependence to humiliate. That this humiliation was experienced by people living on their own ancestral territories, under agreements their leaders had negotiated in good faith, makes it all the more bitter.

The system engineered dependence, then used that dependence to humiliate. Understanding this is essential to understanding what came next: residential schools were not an aberration. They were the logical next step.

— Indigenous History: A Path Forward, Episode 2

The trajectory from treaty-making through the Indian Act to modern self-government is not a neat arc of progress. It is messy, uneven, and incomplete. But it demonstrates something essential: the relationship between Indigenous nations and the Canadian state is not static. It has been renegotiated, contested, and transformed at every stage, and it continues to evolve. The next chapter in this history — residential schools — reveals the most devastating consequence of the framework established in the treaty era: the systematic targeting of Indigenous children as the vehicle for cultural elimination.

Modern Framework

Landmark Modern Treaty Agreements

Selected self-government and comprehensive claims agreements, 1975–2000

James Bay and Northern Quebec Agreement (1975): The first modern land claim agreement, establishing resource-sharing and governance frameworks for Cree and Inuit communities. Nisga’a Final Agreement (1999): The first modern treaty in British Columbia, establishing self-government powers over land, resources, culture, and citizenship. Nunavut Land Claims Agreement (1993) and territory creation (1999): The largest Indigenous land claim settlement in Canadian history, leading to the creation of a new self-governing territory.

Timeline

Key Dates in Treaty History & the Indian Act

From the first Numbered Treaty to the establishment of modern self-government, this timeline traces the critical moments in the long arc of treaty-making and its betrayal.

1871
Treaty 1 (Stone Fort Treaty) Signed

The first of the Numbered Treaties is signed at Lower Fort Garry, Manitoba, establishing $5 annual annuities and reserve allocations for Anishinaabe and Swampy Cree nations. Complaints about unfulfilled commitments begin almost immediately.

1876
The Indian Act Becomes Law

Parliament consolidates earlier colonial legislation into the Indian Act, establishing comprehensive federal control over Indigenous governance, land, identity, and daily life. Indian Agents are installed on reserves as direct representatives of government authority.

1884
Cultural Ceremonies Banned

The potlatch is criminalized, making participation in the foundational governance, economic, and spiritual ceremony of West Coast nations punishable by imprisonment. The Sun Dance and Thirst Dance are subsequently banned on the Plains.

1920
Scott’s Amendment — Compulsory Schooling

Under Duncan Campbell Scott, amendments make residential school attendance compulsory for all Indigenous children. Scott states his objective to eliminate “the Indian problem” through total assimilation into the “body politic.”

1927
Lawyer Ban — Land Claims Blocked

The Indian Act is amended to prohibit Indigenous people from hiring lawyers or raising funds to pursue land claims, effectively eliminating legal recourse against treaty violations for a quarter century.

1951
Indian Act Revisions

Major revisions to the Indian Act lift the potlatch ban and the prohibition on hiring lawyers. However, many core structures of control — the reserve system, band council governance, and the Indian Agent framework — remain intact.

1975
James Bay and Northern Quebec Agreement

Canada’s first modern comprehensive land claims agreement is signed, establishing new frameworks for resource sharing, environmental management, and governance for Cree and Inuit communities in northern Quebec.

1999
Nisga’a Final Agreement

After over a century of advocacy, the Nisga’a Nation signs a comprehensive modern treaty with British Columbia and Canada, establishing self-government and control over lands, resources, language, and culture.

2000
Nunavut Territory Established

The Nunavut territory becomes fully operational with its own legislative assembly, representing the largest Indigenous land claim settlement in Canadian history and a fundamentally new model of governance within Confederation.

Common Questions

Understanding Treaties & the Indian Act

The Numbered Treaties (1871–1921) were a series of eleven agreements between the Crown and Indigenous nations across what is now Canada. Indigenous leaders signed them not out of naivety, but as a pragmatic response to a permanently changed world — the decimation of the buffalo, declining fur trade, and encroaching settlement. They sought a survivable future that included farming tools, education, healthcare, and protection for their children. Critically, Indigenous nations understood treaties as sacred agreements establishing ongoing relationships of mutual obligation, ratified through ceremony, pipe, and verbal commitments — not as one-time land surrenders. The Crown, however, interpreted these same agreements as land transactions that cleared title for settlement and resource extraction.

The Indian Act, passed in 1876 and still in force today, is Canadian federal legislation that governs virtually every aspect of life for Status Indians and reserves. It has been described as a racist and apartheid-like document because it is one of the only pieces of legislation in the world that targets a specific racial group for comprehensive control. The Act installed Indian agents to control reserves, replaced Indigenous governance systems, stripped women of their status if they married non-Indigenous men, banned sacred ceremonies like the potlatch and sun dance, forced children into residential schools, and even prohibited Indigenous peoples from hiring lawyers or raising money to pursue land claims. Deputy Superintendent Duncan Campbell Scott stated in 1920 that its explicit goal was to eliminate the “Indian problem” through total assimilation.

The Indian Act persists because decades of enforced dependency have created a complex situation where some provisions — such as tax exemptions and reserve land protections — are now seen as the only remaining safeguards against further dispossession. Many Indigenous communities fear that repeal without a replacement framework would create a legal vacuum, removing even minimal protections while offering nothing in return. The path forward includes modern treaty-making, self-government agreements such as the Nisga’a Final Agreement and the creation of Nunavut, and custom-of-band elections that gradually replace Indian Act governance with Indigenous-designed systems. The goal is not simple repeal but meaningful replacement — building structures of self-determination that make the Act’s provisions unnecessary.
